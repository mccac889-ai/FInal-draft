When is a painting worth the most?

Using auction records from Van Gogh, Picasso, Basquiat, Klimt and Kahlo, this project asks a slightly morbid question: Is there a “golden window” after an artist’s death when their work explodes in value?

Thesis

Designing a chic obituary for value.

The art market is obsessed with scarcity, provenance and myth. When an artist dies, all three intensify. This site visualizes how long after an artist’s death some of the most expensive works were sold, and experiments with a playful “golden formula” that links time since death to value growth.

This is not a financial tool or serious pricing model. It’s a narrative: how does the market choreograph grief, nostalgia and speculation over decades? Each visualization is a lens on that story, not an absolute truth.

Price vs. afterlife

Who sells for more, and how long after they’re gone?

Bars show headline sale price (USD, millions). Labels show years between the artist’s death and that sale. Van Gogh and Klimt spike over a century posthumously, while Basquiat and Kahlo hit records much sooner.

The “golden” formula

A playful model of value growth after death.

Using this tiny dataset, we fit a simple quadratic curve that assumes value grows after an artist’s death, peaks, then plateaus:

value ≈ 1 + 0.04·t − 0.00025·t² t = years after death, value = relative multiple

Drag the slider to move through time. The dot shows how the model imagines value compounding in the decades after an artist dies. In this toy version, the curve peaks around 70 years after death — close to Frida Kahlo’s current record sale.

Method & limitations

Why this isn’t investment advice.

Data points are drawn from public auction records and museum / encyclopedia entries for a single iconic work per artist. Prices are headline sale prices in USD (including buyer’s premium where reported). “Years after death” is calculated as sale year − artist’s year of death.

With only five data points, any “formula” is speculative. It ignores broader market cycles, private sales, and shifts in taste (for example, renewed attention to women artists like Kahlo). The goal is to create an elegant, legible narrative about how time, death and value are entangled — not to claim causal truth.
